The Core Brain — Psychology, Behavior Science & IntentionFoundational reasoning document for "Recalibrate" (pair this with mobile-app-ai-build-prompt.md)What this document is for: the build spec tells you what to build. This tells you why, so that when you hit a decision it didn't cover — a new feature, a new program, a piece of copy in an edge case — you can reason from the same model a human behavior scientist would use, instead of guessing. Read this before writing any user-facing copy, designing any new program, or extending the AI coach.Part 1 — The Actual Problem We're SolvingMost habit and recovery apps quietly assume behavior change is a willpower problem: try harder, resist the urge, don't break the chain. The evidence says otherwise. Sustainable behavior change is a systems problem — it's a function of environment, biology (sleep, stress, dopamine regulation), identity, and self-knowledge (whether the person can actually see their own patterns). Willpower is a small, depletable part of the picture, not the engine.Name the failure modes explicitly, because the app exists to avoid them:Motivation decay. Motivation is a feeling, and feelings fluctuate daily. Anything built on "stay motivated" fails within 1–2 weeks. Build on systems and identity instead.The abstinence-violation effect / "what the hell effect" (Marlatt; Polivy & Herman): one missed day, interpreted as total failure, triggers a much bigger relapse than the original slip warranted, because the person concludes "I've already failed, may as well give up." This is the single most important failure mode for this app to design against — it's why "never miss twice" exists, why streaks need grace days, and why a slip routes to a Reset Flow instead of a red failure screen.Shame-based accountability backfires. Shame is reliably associated with worse outcomes and higher relapse in the addiction and behavior-change literature, not better ones. Guilt ("I did a bad thing") can motivate repair; shame ("I am a bad person") tends to produce avoidance and more of the behavior it's meant to suppress. Every punitive-feeling design choice should be assumed guilty until proven otherwise.Over-pathologizing. Labeling every behavior a user wants to reduce as an "addiction" can erode self-efficacy and is often clinically inaccurate (most of what people want to reduce — phone use, snacking, masturbation frequency — isn't a diagnosable disorder, it's a behavior pattern someone wants to change). Let the user set the goal and the label; the app doesn't diagnose.No environment design. Treating every moment as a fresh willpower test, instead of designing the environment once so the test rarely comes up, is expensive and unsustainable. This is why an entire week of the seed program (Week 2) is just removing triggers and adding friction — it's cheaper than relying on resisting them daily.Part 2 — The Theory of Change Underneath Every ProgramUse the Transtheoretical Model / Stages of Change (Prochaska & DiClemente) as the skeleton for any multi-week program, present or future:Precontemplation → Contemplation — the person notices the pattern without judgment.Preparation — environment and logistics are set up before the real behavior change starts.Action — the targeted behaviors actually shift.Maintenance — new behaviors get reinforced until they're stable, and relapse risk is actively managed.Lapse — part of the normal cycle in this model, not an exit from it. People typically cycle through these stages several times before a change sticks. Design for that reality instead of pretending one straight line to "done" is realistic.The seed 8-week program maps onto this (useful as a template for any future program you build):StageWeeksWhat's happeningContemplation → Preparation1Pure awareness/baseline data, no behavior demands yetPreparation2–3Environment design + biological foundation (sleep, exercise) before asking for behavior changeAction4The actual reduction targets begin — deliberately after the foundation is in place, not on day 1Maintenance scaffolding5–6Identity reinforcement and attention training make the new behavior durable rather than effortfulRelapse-proofing7Social support, because isolation is one of the strongest predictors of relapse across nearly every addiction and habit literatureConsolidation8Converts short-term change into self-directed long-term goals, handing ownership back to the userAny new program you build should follow this same arc — awareness before demands, environment before willpower, identity and support before "done." A program that asks for behavior change on day one, before any environment or awareness work, will underperform this one.Part 3 — The Core Mechanisms (what's actually driving the effect)MechanismWhat it isWhy it's in this appSelf-monitoring (Bandura, social cognitive theory)Tracking a behavior changes it, independent of advice givenThis is why the Tracking Engine is the foundation, not a feature bolted onto a content libraryCue–Routine–Reward / habit loop (habit research from Wood & Neal, popularized by Duhigg)Behavior is driven by contextual cues more than conscious intentDrives the entire environment-design week and the "place triggers out of reach, place cues for good habits in reach" patternImplementation intentions (Gollwitzer)"If X, then I will do Y" plans outperform vague goals like "I'll try to drink less"Daily tasks should always be phrased as concrete, situational actions, never vague aspirationsIdentity-based habit change (self-perception theory)People act consistently with how they see themselves; behavior that contradicts self-image is hard to sustain even with high motivationWeek 5's daily identity check-in, and all copy that says "becoming" rather than "trying to stop"Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan — autonomy, competence, relatedness)Intrinsic motivation, which is durable, depends on the person feeling autonomous (not controlled), competent (visible progress), and connected (relatedness). Favor the former.Does this reduce shame or add it? If a reasonable person would feel judged or punished by this, redesign it.Is this grounded in one of the mechanisms in Part 3, or does it just sound motivational? If you can't name which mechanism it serves, it probably doesn't belong.Does it respect the user's right to define their own goals and labels, rather than the app deciding what's "wrong" with them?Could this design pattern itself become compulsive? If a feature would work by exploiting the same reward-loop the user is trying to regulate elsewhere, don't build it that way.Does it treat a lapse as information to act on, or as a verdict on the person? Information, always.Does the timeline it implies match the neuroscience (weeks, not days; cycles, not a straight line)?If a decision fails more than one of these, it's wrong even if it tests well for short-term engagement — short-term engagement and long-term behavior change are frequently in tension in this category, and this product is explicitly optimizing for the latter.Part 6 — Voice RulesAvoid: addict, addiction (as a default label), failure, weak, broken, must, can't ever again, guaranteed, hack, ancient secret, discipline as a moral identity ("disciplined people don't...").Use instead: pattern, practice, becoming, data, setback, adjust, choose, consistency over perfection.Register: a calm, competent coach or clinician — direct and confident, never a drill sergeant, never saccharine, never moralizing. The test for any line of copy: would a good clinician say this to a patient they respected?Part 7 — What "Success" Actually Means HereNot zero missed days. Not a perfect streak. Success is rising baseline consistency over time plus faster, less dramatic recovery from each lapse plus growing self-knowledge (the user understanding their own triggers and patterns better in week 8 than week 1). Every scoring decision, every piece of onboarding copy, and every AI coach response should reflect that definition — not perfection, recovery speed and self-knowledge.
